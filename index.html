Color blindness

Color blindness (color vision deficiency) is the decreased ability to see color or differences in color. It can impair tasks such as selecting ripe fruit, choosing clothing, and reading traffic lights. The most common cause of color blindness is an inherited problem in the development of one or more of the three sets of the eyes' cone cells, which sense color. There are three main types of color blindness going under the name of dichromacy, which means only two different color receptors (cones) are in the retina instead of three:

  • Protanopia : less sensitivity to red light because the red cone is missing, whereas the green and the blue cones are present
  • Deuteranopia: less sensitivity to green light because the green cone is missing, whereas the red and the blue cones are present
  • Tritanopia: less sensitivity to blue light because the blue cone is missing, whereas the red and the green cones are present

Other three type of color blindness (protanomaly, deuteranomaly, and tritanomaly) go under the classification of anomalous trichromacy, which means all three different color receptors (cones) are present but one of them is shifted in its peak.

For more information see Wikipedia.
